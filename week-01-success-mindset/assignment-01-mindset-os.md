# Assignment 1. What is something you believe to be true that most people around you would disagree with?

### Rules

* No "safe" answers.
* Must be your real belief (not copied from internet).
* Minimum 50 words.

**Hint:** What do you believe about career, money, learning, discipline, relationships, health, success, life, tech industry, etc. that most people don't agree with?

## Answer

I believe the concept of religion has overtaken our quest for purpose and drive to be more. Instead of pushing people to take ownership of their choices, build discipline, and chase growth on their own terms, it often hands them a ready-made explanation for why things are the way they are and tells them to simply have faith. I've watched it eat people's ambition, their willingness to question things, and their sense of personal responsibility. Whenever I try to bring this up, most people don't agree with me, and a lot of them get defensive instead of actually engaging with the point.

# What My 2.0 Version Looked Like (2029–2033)

There was a time when Olatunde Ibitoye was known as a software engineer who enjoyed building applications. Today, he is recognized as an engineer who builds the systems that allow companies to scale products confidently.

Over the last several years, he established himself at the intersection of Software Engineering, Agentic AI, DevOps, Cloud Engineering, and Platform Architecture. His work became less about writing isolated features and more about designing reliable platforms that powered AI products used by thousands—and eventually millions—of users.

His career accelerated after making a deliberate decision to specialize instead of remaining a generalist. While many engineers focused solely on building applications, he focused on building the infrastructure, automation, and intelligent systems that allowed those applications to reach production quickly, safely, and repeatedly.

He joined leading AI startups, where he led the design and implementation of cloud-native infrastructure, CI/CD pipelines, Kubernetes platforms, observability systems, security automation, and AI deployment workflows. He became known for transforming complex engineering problems into scalable, maintainable systems that engineering teams could trust.

Several products he architected successfully moved from prototype to production. Those products served customers across multiple regions while maintaining high availability, security, and performance. His contributions significantly reduced deployment time, improved infrastructure reliability, lowered operational costs, and enabled engineering teams to ship new features with confidence.

His GitHub profile evolved into a public portfolio of production-grade engineering projects rather than tutorial repositories. It showcased:

* Enterprise DevOps architectures
* AI agent deployment platforms
* Multi-cloud infrastructure projects
* Kubernetes production clusters
* Platform engineering tools
* Internal developer platforms
* Payment infrastructure integrations
* Secure CI/CD pipelines
* Infrastructure as Code using Terraform
* Cloud automation projects
* Event-driven microservices
* AI-powered operational tooling

Every repository documented real engineering decisions, architectural trade-offs, monitoring strategies, scalability patterns, and deployment practices.

His technical writing became another defining part of his career.

Rather than publishing generic programming tutorials, he wrote detailed engineering articles explaining how backend engineering, cloud infrastructure, DevOps, AI agents, and software architecture work together inside modern technology companies.

His articles explored topics such as:

* Scaling AI applications in production
* Building resilient cloud architectures
* Agentic AI operations
* Platform engineering best practices
* Engineering behind payment systems
* Designing observable distributed systems
* Cost optimization in cloud infrastructure
* DevSecOps implementation strategies
* Infrastructure reliability engineering
* AI deployment architectures

These publications attracted thousands of engineers globally and established him as a trusted technical educator.

He also became a regular conference speaker, engineering mentor, and open-source contributor. Through workshops, technical talks, and community mentorship, he helped hundreds of aspiring engineers transition into careers in backend engineering, DevOps, cloud engineering, and AI infrastructure.

His leadership extended beyond writing code.

He mentored engineering teams, reviewed architectural decisions, established engineering standards, and built internal developer platforms that improved developer productivity across organizations. He became the engineer people trusted when systems needed to scale without compromising reliability.

Along the way, he earned advanced cloud and DevOps certifications, contributed to widely used open-source projects, completed specialized training in platform engineering and AI infrastructure, and continuously invested in learning emerging technologies.

Financially and professionally, his consistency paid off.

He worked with some of the world's leading AI startups, helping transform innovative ideas into production-ready products capable of serving customers at scale. His expertise became valuable not only because he could build software, but because he understood the complete lifecycle—from architecture and infrastructure to deployment, observability, automation, security, and long-term scalability.

Outside work, he became known for giving back to the engineering community.

He openly documented his journey, shared lessons from production incidents, published architecture case studies, reviewed resumes and portfolios, mentored young engineers, and encouraged others who felt overwhelmed by the rapidly changing technology landscape.

Those who met him often remarked that despite his achievements, he remained approachable and genuinely invested in helping others grow.

His greatest accomplishment was not the companies he worked for or the technologies he mastered. It was proving that disciplined learning, consistent execution, and a willingness to share knowledge could transform an ordinary software engineer into an engineering leader trusted to build the infrastructure behind the next generation of AI products.

The future he envisioned years earlier was no longer an aspiration.

It had become his reality.

# Reflection – Week 1

### Biggest insight I got about myself this week

I realized that I don't lack potential or ability,  I lack a clear system for consistently executing on my goals. When I define a compelling vision and break it down into daily actions, I become more focused and confident. I also discovered that I enjoy solving meaningful technical problems and teaching others through the things I build and share.

### My biggest weakness/loop I noticed

I tend to spend too much time planning, researching, and learning without shipping enough work. This creates a cycle where I feel productive but don't always produce visible results. I've realized that consistent execution and public proof of work are more valuable than waiting until everything feels perfect.

### One system I will implement from this week (exact habit + time)

3 hours of uninterrupted deep work on DMI assignments
1 hour documenting what I learned.
30 minutes creating or scheduling a LinkedIn post, my video or any other thing.
